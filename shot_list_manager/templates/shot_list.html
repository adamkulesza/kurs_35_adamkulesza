Camera Shot type

Extreme Wide Shot / Distant Shot:
Presents the general topography of the action space (panorama). The human figure is small, blended into a vast background, or entirely absent. Typically used for general descriptions of the setting or for showcasing landscapes.

Wide Shot / Establishing Shot:
Shows a full view of the location where the action takes place. The human figure is visible, and the background or set is clearly shown. It usually serves to establish the relationship between characters and their environment and to convey general information about the setting.

Full Shot:
A cropped version of the wide shot, framing the entire human figure head-to-toe. Part of the background or set is still visible. Common in silent cinema.

Medium Long Shot / American Shot:
A portion of the full shot, showing the character from the knees up. The figure dominates the frame and it’s most often used in dialogue scenes.

Medium Shot (MS):
Frames the character from the waist up, placing more emphasis on facial expressions than the American shot.

Medium Close-Up (Half Close-Up):
Frames the character from the chest or shoulders up. The subject dominates the background.

Close-Up / Big Close-Up:
The face or another object takes up a large part or all of the frame, allowing for detailed observation of expressions or features.

Detail Shot / Extreme Close-Up:
A specific type of close-up that focuses on a particular body part or a key object, filling the entire frame. It maximizes viewer attention and is often used to increase emotional tension.

Macro Detail Shot:
Presents a fragment of reality not visible to the naked eye.


Other Specialized Shots

Over-the-Shoulder (OTS): Common in conversations. Shows one character’s point of view over another’s shoulder.
Two-shot: Includes two people in the same frame, often used in dialogue scenes.
Point of View Shot (POV): Shows exactly what a character sees.
Tracking/Dolly Shot: Camera physically moves through the scene, following the subject.
Crane Shot: Camera moves vertically, often to reveal or emphasize a large area or dramatic moment.
Handheld Shot: Shaky, naturalistic movement. Feels raw or documentary-like.
Static Shot: Camera stays still—no panning or zooming.
Basic Camera Movements

Pan:
Horizontal movement of the camera from a fixed position (left or right).
Often used to follow action or reveal surroundings.

Tilt:
Vertical movement of the camera from a fixed position (up or down).
Often used to show height, scale, or emphasize a character’s power.

Zoom
Changing the focal length of the lens to make the subject appear closer or farther.
Not a physical movement, but creates the illusion of moving in or out.
Can be zoom in or zoom out.

Dolly (Tracking Shot)
The camera physically moves forward/backward or side to side on a dolly (wheeled platform).
Smooth and dynamic; can follow characters or push in for emphasis.

Truck (Side Dolly)
Lateral movement of the camera (left or right) along a track or smooth surface.
Often used to follow a moving subject or reveal surroundings.

Pedestal (or Boom Up/Down)
The entire camera moves vertically up or down, often using a crane or jib.
Used to follow a subject vertically or reveal the scale of a setting.

Advanced/Creative Camera Movements

Crane Shot / Jib Shot
Camera is lifted high using a crane or jib arm.
Can sweep over or above the scene for dramatic effect.

Steadicam Shot
A camera stabilizer system allows for smooth movement while walking or running.
Fluid but mobile; used for tracking shots, especially through tight or complex spaces.

Handheld Shot
The camera is held by the operator without a stabilizer.
Creates a shaky, realistic, or documentary-style look.

Rack Focus (Focus Pull)
A shift in focus from one subject to another in the same shot.
Directs the viewer’s attention from foreground to background (or vice versa)

360-Degree Shot The camera moves in a full circle around the subject.
Creates dynamic perspective and tension, often used in action or introspective scenes.

Whip Pan (Swish Pan)
A very fast pan, often used for transitions or to show sudden motion.
Creates a blur effect and adds energy.
Push-In / Pull-Out
A slow dolly-in toward the subject (push-in) or away (pull-out).
Used to build emotion, tension, or emphasize a moment.
